Kindergarten Curriculum

Core elements of our program

Imaginative Learning

Free, imaginative play and purposeful work — kneading bread, sweeping, building, caring for the room. Through meaningful play, children learn to think, solve, and do.

Whole-brain, whole child

The brain's core architecture is wired in the early years. Our program builds rich connections across attention, memory, regulation, and language — developing the whole child, body and mind.

Music & the arts

Singing, painting, modeling, handwork — color, song, and story are woven through every day. The arts build focus, fine motor skills, and a lifelong love of beauty.

Growing in community

Reading others, working through conflict, steadying big feelings. In a mixed-age classroom where teachers model calm, children develop the social fluency and self-regulation that last a lifetime.

Nature & the farm

Digging, planting, climbing, tending animals — outdoors in all weather, across every season. On the farm and beyond, children grow a rooted connection to the living world.

Wonder & connection

Wonder, merriment, awe — these are the gifts of early childhood, and we work to preserve them. Every child is known, met with patience, and woven into a community that cares for them.

Our Kindergarten

A gentle step into a wider world

The kindergarten years carry a child from early childhood toward the threshold of formal learning. We've shaped every detail of the day — and every corner of the classroom — to honor what that passage asks of them.

Preparing for learning, the Waldorf way

We don't rush academics; we ready the child for them — building the attention, memory, language, and fine-motor capacities that reading and arithmetic will later rest upon.

The gift of mixed ages in TK and Kindergarten

Younger children learn by looking up to the elders; older children grow in confidence and care by guiding the younger. The mixed-age room becomes a small, working community.

Connection to nature

Long, unhurried hours outdoors in every season, beside a working biodynamic farm — feeding chickens, gathering eggs, pulling carrots the children helped plant.

Festivals and the life of the community

Seasonal festivals gather families across the year — a living web that holds your child, and you, well beyond the classroom.

Early Childhood

The neuroscience of our early childhood approach

Early childhood is a period of rapid brain development. In these years the brain forms connections faster than at any later stage — laying the architecture on which all later learning is built. Every part of our day is chosen to strengthen the systems that foundation depends on.

The systems we intentionally strengthen

Executive Function & Attention

Focus, working memory, impulse control, flexible thinking — supported by open-ended play, real responsibilities, and activities that require planning and persistence.

Emotional Regulation

Processing emotion, managing stress, returning to a regulated state — capacities shaped by warm relationship and an unhurried, predictable rhythm.

Sensory Integration

Organizing touch, movement, balance, and spatial awareness — the foundational capacity that underlies attention, regulation, and later academic skill.

Motor Control

Movement, balance, coordination, fine motor control. In early childhood, movement is a primary mechanism of learning, not a break from it.

Language & Communication

Comprehension, expression, vocabulary, symbolic thinking — developed through conversation, storytelling, and song rather than rote instruction.

Social Cognition

Empathy, perspective-taking, collaboration — established through direct social experience rather than explicit instruction.

Memory

Encoding and retrieving meaningful experience. Early memory is supported by emotional salience, context, and narrative rather than repetition.

Reward & Motivation

Curiosity, engagement, intrinsic motivation. Experiences of mastery and agency reinforce the association between effort and reward.

Common Questions

Frequently asked questions

Does Summerfield offer TK and Kindergarten?

Yes. Our Kindergarten serves children ages 4–6, covering what would elsewhere be considered both Transitional Kindergarten (TK) and Kindergarten, in a mixed-age classroom.

What does a typical kindergarten day look like?

A steady, gentle rhythm: outdoor free play, morning circle with songs and verses, creative and practical activity, a story told by the teacher, a shared meal, and time on the farm and in the gardens.

Why don't you accelerate academics in kindergarten?

Early skill drilling can produce short-term gains that fade by second or third grade. Rather than delay learning, we sequence it — building the play, movement, and language foundation that durable later learning rests on.

Where is Summerfield Waldorf School located?

On a biodynamic farm in Santa Rosa, California, in the heart of Sonoma County, with daily connection to nature as part of the school experience.

Where do Summerfield families come from?

From across West Sonoma County — Sebastopol, Forestville, Occidental, Graton, Guerneville, Windsor, Healdsburg, Rohnert Park — and beyond.

What age should my child be to enroll in kindergarten?

Our Kindergarten welcomes children ages 4–6. We're glad to talk through readiness with you on a tour.

What is the tuition for Summerfield Kindergarten (2026–2027)?

Tuition varies by program (4- or 5-day). We'll share current figures and discuss the right fit during your visit; tuition assistance is available for Kindergarten through 12th grade.

What are the health and immunization requirements for kindergarten?

All children must be up to date on age-appropriate immunizations before enrollment and throughout the year, in accordance with California state law.

Do you provide meals and snacks?

Yes — wholesome meals and snacks each day, from simple recipes refined over fifty years, with ingredients often fresh from our school farm.

Do you offer summer programs for kindergarten-aged children?

Yes — our Farm Camp offers a gentle, nature-rich summer of crafts, song, storytelling, and play on the Farm and in the Permaculture Garden.

Can we visit or tour the campus?

Yes — a tour is the best way to feel the warmth and rhythm of the kindergarten firsthand. You'll see the classrooms, the outdoor spaces, and meet our faculty.
